# DEVLOG

## Phase 0: scaffold and schema

Set up the skeleton and locked the schema before writing any pipeline code, because the schema is the thing that forces rework if I get it wrong.

First I pulled the working docs and secrets out of the tree the public repo tracks. The env file and the four planning notes now sit at the root but are ignored, and I fixed the ignore file so two of them that were slipping through stay local along with anything zipped. This repo is public, so that check comes before anything else.

Then the schema. I wrote it straight from the plan as runnable Postgres, one file, dimensions before facts. The two things I was careful about: the date split, where the sold date is null for Grailed and only eBay's ended date drives anything time-based, plus a lower-bound listing date for Grailed; and a per-source condition table, since Grailed and eBay describe wear with different words and I never want those joined on raw text. I applied it to Neon and confirmed the tables landed.

One thing I already know will bite the adapter work if I forget it: the Grailed fixtures come in two shapes. The full export carries an id, sold flag, dates, strata, and category. The overview export is stripped to about ten fields with no id and no dates. The id is recoverable from the listing url, and every field the stripped shape lacks is nullable, so the schema holds without a change. The adapter will tolerate both and I will re-export the sparse ones in full when I pull the real corpus.

A couple of local calls worth noting. psql is not on this machine, so I apply the schema through a small Python runner instead; the plain psql command still works for anyone who has it. And I keep dependencies in a project venv rather than the base environment.

## Phase 1: ingestion and normalization

Wrote one adapter per source, each an anti-corruption layer: raw rows in, canonical rows out, and nothing source-specific allowed past that line. The six data rules are enforced right at that boundary rather than cleaned up later.

eBay was the fussy one. Prices arrive as strings, so I cast them at the edge and reject anything that will not parse instead of quietly turning it into a zero, which would poison every median downstream. Best-offer sales get flagged rather than dropped, since the shown price can understate the real one and the comps layer should be free to down-weight them. eBay carries the only trustworthy sold date, so it is the one source that feeds anything time-based; Grailed's sold timestamp is scrape time and stays null.

Grailed needed to swallow both export shapes. The full run has an id, a sold flag, dates, strata and a category; the overview export has about ten fields and none of that. I recover the listing id from the url and treat overview rows as sold, since every Grailed run is a sold-only search and the sold price differs from the ask on most of them. When I pull the real corpus I will re-export those in full so nothing leans on that assumption.

Condition and size are mapped per source and never joined on raw text, because Gently Used and Pre-Owned are not the same word and should not be forced together. The schema gives those two small dimensions no unique key, so the loader does an application-level get-or-create with an in-process cache to stay idempotent, while the facts dedup on their natural key with an on-conflict-do-nothing. I load the fact rows in a single batched statement rather than row by row, which drops a full corpus load from a crawl to a few seconds over the network.

Loaded the fixtures into Neon and checked the rules held in the database, not just in the tests: Grailed has no sold date, eBay's dates span the real window, the best-offer count matches the sample, conditions stay split by source, and no price is null. Running the load twice inserts nothing the second time. Adapter tests run against the committed fixtures offline; the one test that touches Neon is opt-in so the default suite stays fast.

## Phase 2: entity resolution, text first

Built a curated taxonomy by hand (brand aliases, per-brand model names, an archetype vocabulary, season-code patterns) and a staged resolver on top of it. It reads brand first, then archetype and model, then the season code. A piece only forms when I have the brand plus at least an archetype or a model. A row that resolves to a brand and nothing else is left unresolved rather than folded into one giant per-brand bucket, because that bucket would poison every comp that reads it. I also treat the seed query as a brand prior, since each run is brand-specific and plenty of titles drop the brand and lead with a model instead.

First numbers on the fixtures: brand resolves on every row, a piece gets assigned on about ninety one percent to start, model on about half, season on fifteen. eBay resolves deeper because its search term usually carries the model; Grailed carries the season codes. One piece matched across both marketplaces, a Rick Owens Ramones, which is the entire point of the exercise, though these fixtures barely overlap across sources so I cannot show that at scale yet.

The rows that did not resolve are the interesting part. They are not brand misses. They are collaborations where the garment word belongs to the other label, plus garment words my vocabulary did not carry yet like vest and jersey, plus a few model names I had not seeded. That tells me the gap is coverage, not meaning, so title embeddings would not earn their weight here. I widened the curated vocabulary instead, which lifted the piece rate to about ninety five percent, and I am holding embeddings as a fallback for a larger real pull that shows a genuine semantic gap.

Two things in the leftover rows are worth flagging rather than chasing. First, several are graphic or collection pieces with no garment word at all, which the curated route will never catch cleanly. Second, the eBay search for Carol Christian Poell returned womenswear from a different label spelled Carole Christian, and because I lean on the search term as a brand hint those got tagged with the archive brand even though the title never confirms it. They stay unresolved, so they do not reach any comp, but it is a reminder to tighten that seed query and to prefer a brand the title actually corroborates over one the search term merely implies. Adding a dress archetype to rescue them would be exactly wrong for a menswear project.

## Phase 3: marts

Built the analytics in dbt. Staging views sit over the canonical tables, one intermediate model joins each sale to its resolved piece and its marketplace, and four marts sit on top of that. The date split is enforced here and not just talked about: the comps mart reads every sold row from both sources because price level is undated, while the velocity mart is eBay only because eBay is the one source with a sold date I trust.

The comps mart is the centre of gravity. Per piece it gives the median and the tenth and ninetieth percentiles, a count, and a grade from A to D driven by how many comps stand behind it. When a piece is too thin to trust on its own it falls back to a brand-and-archetype median, so a two-comp number is never dressed up as authoritative. It also carries a second median with best-offer sales removed, next to the all-in one, so the read layer can decide how much to trust the negotiated prices. On the seed data the deep pieces come out sensible: Rick Owens Ramones at fifty one comps and a three hundred dollar median graded A, Margiela GATs and Futures close behind. Most pieces are grade D on one or two comps, which is simply what archive resale looks like and the whole reason the grade sits next to every number.

The cross-marketplace mart has a single row today, the Ramones again, and it shows Grailed running about seventeen percent under eBay for that shoe. Tiny sample, but the join works and it fills in as more pieces resolve across both sources. Velocity is honest about its ceiling: eBay sold comps carry no listing-start date, so there is no days-to-sell, only a sold-per-week rate over the window I actually observed. The markdown mart reads the Grailed ask-to-sold gap and lands where you would expect, low double digits.

dbt tests cover the grain, the not-null keys, the allowed confidence grades, and referential integrity back to the piece table, and they pass. One snag on the way in: an unpinned dbt install pulled a core prerelease that could not load the postgres adapter, so I pinned it to a stable release.

## Phase 4: the site

Built the front end as a thin read layer and kept it that way. Server components query the marts straight from Neon and render; there is no logic in the browser and no logic in the frontend at all beyond formatting. Search takes you to a piece, and the piece page lays out the sold distribution as a histogram with the median and the tenth and ninetieth percentiles marked, the recommended list price with its confidence grade and a note on whether it came from the piece itself or the brand-and-archetype fallback, the eBay velocity, and the cross-marketplace spread.

Every panel says what it cannot show. No dated eBay sales means no velocity block, a piece that only sold in one place means no spread, and the best-offer count sits right under the distribution next to the reliable-only median so nobody reads a negotiated price as a clean one.

I verified this by running it against Neon, not by trusting the build. That mattered. The production build compiled with no complaints, but the running app threw a five hundred on any piece that had velocity, because Postgres returns date columns as date objects and React refuses to render those. A typecheck sails right past it; driving the actual page is what caught it. Casting the dates to text at the query fixed it and the Ramones page came up clean, three hundred median, Grailed running under eBay, the histogram with its percentile lines. I also moved off a Next version with a security advisory and pinned a lockfile so the deploy is reproducible. The deploy itself waits on the Vercel account, so everything is staged and handed over rather than pushed.

## Phase 5: the price model, and being honest about it

Trained a LightGBM regressor on the resolved sold rows. Features are the categoricals that survive the boundary, brand, archetype, model, season, marketplace, condition, and the best-offer flag, and the target is the USD price on a log scale. The part I cared about most was scoring it honestly. I used group cross-validation by piece, so the model is always judged on pieces it never saw. Splitting rows at random would let it memorize a piece's price and post a flattering number; grouping by piece is the test that actually means something.

Every metric prints next to a plain brand-and-archetype median baseline, and the verdict is that the model does not beat the median in any way that matters. Mean absolute error lands around two hundred forty dollars either way, a single percent of lift. That is the truth on a few hundred sales that skew heavily to footwear with most pieces carrying one or two comps, and I would rather say it plainly than dress it up. The comps mart already recommends off that same median, so nothing is lost by admitting the model has no edge yet. Importance sits where you'd expect, marketplace and condition and brand carry it, while archetype and season add nothing because they are mostly redundant with brand or too thin to matter. The model earns its keep only after the corpus is broadened and deepened, which is the whole reason that broadening sits at the front of the plan.

The arbitrage detector is written and tested but deliberately inert. It flags an active ask sitting under a piece's tenth percentile, and only for pieces with at least three comps so a single sale can never trigger it. It stays empty until an active-listing pull is approved, because that pull spends.

## The first real corpus

Pulled the real thing at last. Thirty archive and avant-garde menswear brands across both marketplaces, driven off the actual store brand list rather than my original dozen. Grailed runs one search per brand; eBay I had to learn the hard way, its actor rejects more than six keywords a run, so I chunk them. The seed of a few hundred rows became about thirty six hundred, and the pulled Grailed rows came back in the full twenty nine field shape, which confirms the stripped fixtures were only ever a bad export.

I widened the taxonomy to the whole store list, near eighty brands, and switched brand matching to whole words so a three letter code like roa or lv cannot fire inside a longer word. The payoff is the part I care about. Resolution assigns a piece to about eighty six percent of rows, distinct pieces went from eighty six to four hundred ten, and pieces that sold on both marketplaces went from one to a hundred seventy two. That last number is the whole point of the project, the same piece priced in two places, and it only shows up once the corpus is wide enough for the sources to overlap. The archetype mix is no longer footwear, it spreads across tops, outerwear, denim, knit, pants and the rest, which was the reason for broadening in the first place. When the bigger corpus exposed more garment words my vocabulary lacked, loafers, chinos, suit, jumper, I added them; coverage, not meaning, the same call as before.

One thing I will not dress up. At brand-plus-archetype granularity the cross-marketplace spreads are noisy. A nine dollar eBay item and a two hundred dollar Grailed item can both land in vetements accessory and manufacture a six hundred percent spread that means nothing, and the same low eBay noise drags some piece medians down. The median holds up better than the spread because it is a median, but the honest read is that these coarse fallback pieces conflate different objects. The fix is finer, model-level resolution and price sanity fences on the marts, and it is a deliberate next step rather than something I will paper over now.

Kept the committed fixtures frozen so the test suite stays deterministic; the real corpus lives in data/raw, which is ignored, and in Neon. Tests and marts are green. The pull cost about what I said it would, in the low double digits.

## Hardening the marts, and being wrong twice

The broadened corpus made the cross-marketplace spread mart embarrassing. At brand-plus-archetype granularity a nine dollar eBay sticker was being averaged against a two hundred dollar Grailed bag, so the mart cheerfully reported spreads of several thousand percent. So I hardened it: only compare pieces resolved to a specific model, require real comps on both sides, cap the gap, and add a small cleaning step to drop junk and obvious fakes. It built green and the worst spread dropped from three thousand percent to under a hundred, and I nearly wrote it up as done.

Then I had it reviewed, hard, by a few independent passes, and they caught three real mistakes I had shipped. The first was mine and it was bad: my fake filter matched the word replica as a bare substring, which deleted Maison Margiela's actual Replica line and Raf's Replicant, forty two genuine listings, and caught not a single real counterfeit. Worse, it stripped the cheaper Replica GATs unevenly and inflated my headline GAT spread from a true seventy three percent to a fake ninety seven. The clean step was manufacturing the very number it was supposed to protect. The second was the price floor: a flat twenty dollar cutoff is a one-sided trim, so it pushed every affected median upward and quietly dropped real cheap sales like a Uniqlo collab and a Fred Perry Raf sweater. The third was deeper. A model label does not guarantee one object, because the resolver was reading the model off the search keyword, so a search for rick owens ramones that returned Geobaskets stamped Ramones on all of them and built a grab-bag piece whose spread was meaningless.

I fixed all three. The fake filter is gone; it caught nothing real and deleted plenty. The floor dropped to five dollars, enough to kill stickers and listing errors without biasing the medians. And the resolver now takes the model from the title only, never the keyword, which splits the Geobaskets out of the Ramones piece into their own. After that the numbers are honest: Margiela Replica is back, the Julius median sits at its real thirty two rather than an inflated seventy, the Ramones spread is now a clean Ramones against clean Ramones, and the GAT spread reads its true seventy three percent on the full comp set. The spread cap is symmetric now so eBay-high contamination cannot slip through the way Grailed-high could not.

What I did not pretend to solve: the spread mart is only five rows, because only a handful of pieces are resolved to a model with enough comps on both marketplaces, and that grows only as model-level resolution deepens. And a brand can still be contaminated at the source, a search for the label Julius pulls in Julius Erving and Paul Frank's Julius the Monkey, which no price fence fixes and which belongs to brand disambiguation, not here. The honest headline is that the tool now reports five cross-marketplace spreads it can stand behind instead of a hundred and seventy it could not, and that the review was right to make me redo it.

One environment note. This working copy lives in an iCloud-synced folder, and the Python environment sitting inside it made tool startup stall for minutes at a time while the sync layer woke cold files. I moved the environment out of the synced tree and turned off test-plugin autoloading, since there are no third-party plugins to load, and runs behave again.

## The website begins: ReliQuery, and one rule

Started the site that sits on top of all this, and rebuilt it from scratch rather than carrying the proof of concept forward. It reads the marts and renders them, nothing more; no pricing or analytics logic lives in the frontend. The name is the whole design idea. Reli is set in an archival serif, Query in a technical monospace, and that split becomes the one rule the site obeys everywhere: if a thing is a relic it is serif, brand names and piece names and the prose, and if a thing is a measurement it is mono, every price and count and percentile and season code. The rule does real work. A number reads as data because it is visually marked as data, and I enforced the convention inside each component so it is structural rather than something to remember per use.

The old frontend was a plain proof of concept, so I threw it out and scaffolded fresh. Next on the App Router, the three faces loaded and self-hosted through next/font, visx for the charts that come later, postgres.js for the read layer. This first pass is the design system and the shell only, nothing wired to data yet: the color and type and spacing and motion tokens exactly as specified, a fixed grain layer at a few percent, the nav and footer and grid, the base components, and a styleguide route that renders every one of them in isolation so the system can be judged on its own before a single page exists. I built only what the marts actually support, routed pieces on the stable canonical key rather than the serial id that churns on every resolver run, and treated imagery as an enhancement since no piece image reaches a mart yet. The wordmark took the most fiddling. The mono runs wide and heavy next to the serif, so Query wanted to shout over Reli; I dropped its size and weight, pulled the tracking in and nudged the baseline until the two read as one word.

Then I had the design system reviewed the same way I review the data work, and it earned its keep. The worst thing it found was in my own hands: the missing-number placeholder in my formatters was an em dash, the one character the house voice bans everywhere, sitting in the code that renders every absent price. Gone. It also caught that my dim labels and, worse, my confidence grade letters had slipped under readable contrast; a grade D you cannot read is a grade that does no work, so I lifted the low grades a step while keeping the ink-weight descent that reads weak from strong. The confidence badge needed a role before a screen reader would announce it at all. The sticky nav blur was missing its webkit prefix, the frosted surfaces needed solid fallbacks under them, and the database client now initializes lazily so a missing connection string cannot throw during a build that never touches the database.

Two things I did not paper over. The styleguide names what is still deferred, the price bands and the distribution and the method scrollytelling and the hero, so it cannot read as more finished than it is. And I left one tension for a later call rather than settling it silently: the plan deliberately dims the low-confidence grades toward the background, which is the right instinct and also fights contrast, so I met it halfway and flagged it for a proper decision. Local only, no deploy. The next step is the piece page, the one that has to prove the whole thing.

## The piece page, and reading the schema before writing a line

Built the page the whole project points at: what a piece is worth, where, how fast, and what to list it at. Before writing a single query I read the marts as they actually are, not as the plan imagines them, and that paid for itself at once. There is no canonical_key column, so I derive the routing key in the read layer from the four fields the resolver dedups on, which keeps a shared URL alive across re-resolution even as the serial piece id churns underneath. The spread and markdown columns are ratios, not percents, so they get multiplied at the render boundary. And the date columns come back from the driver as date objects, the exact thing that threw a five hundred on the old front end, so every date is cast to text in the query. I would not have known any of that from the plan.

The page lays the answer out one rule-separated section at a time. A recommendation block with its confidence grade and the sample it rests on, and a thin-data notice in the open when the number falls back to a brand-and-archetype median. A price band with a best-offer toggle that recomputes the percentiles from the fetched rows, sitting above a distribution that keeps every sale and hatches the negotiated ones. Velocity that says plainly it is eBay-only and has no days-to-sell, because eBay carries the only trusted date. A markdown magnitude, not a decay curve. A cross-marketplace spread for the handful of pieces that earn one, with graceful absence as the default. And the comps underneath, mono throughout, Grailed rows marked undated rather than given an invented date, the real listing titles shown warts and all. The charts are visx scales with hand-drawn marks so I own every hairline, server-rendered where they can be, each with an aria label and a data-table fallback so the analysis is not locked inside a canvas. Search fetches the whole small corpus once and filters in the browser, with brand and archetype and season chips and a sort. The method page is the honest one: the pipeline in a line, the hard part of resolving one piece across two marketplaces made concrete with a real matched example, and the model shown against the plain median baseline it does not beat, labeled as a snapshot because those figures are printed to stdout, not stored.

One honest note on how I checked it. For this whole stretch the machine could not start the dev server or finish a production build; the working copy sits in an iCloud folder, and the sync layer waking cold dependency files starves Next of the worker processes it forks, the same drag that hit the Python side earlier. So I verified the way that finds bugs rather than the way that looks clean. I typechecked, I ran the real query SQL against Neon and confirmed every cast and the null-key matching and the numeric ordering, which is the same check that caught the date five hundred last time and now shows dates coming back as strings, and I had the whole thing reviewed hard against the real schema. The review found no schema mismatches and a short list of edge cases I fixed: a best-offer toggle that mislabeled itself when every comp on a piece was a best-offer sale, two numbers set in the body face instead of mono, and a price sort that was quietly lexicographic behind a text cast. The animated search-to-detail transition, the cross-highlighting, and the WebGL hero are the polish pass. The market page stays cut until it has rollup marts to stand on.

## The polish pass, and making motion optional

Went back over the finished pages and added the motion the plan held for last. The recommended price and the landing stats count up once as they come into view, sections rise in on scroll, hovering a comp row lights its bucket in the distribution and hovering a bucket lights its rows, and moving from a search result into a piece uses a view transition where the browser supports one. The hero carries a slow generative point field behind the wordmark, archival dust, or a scatter of comps settling. I built that field in canvas rather than WebGL. The plan named react-three-fiber, but the effect wants no third dimension, three.js is a heavy dependency this machine cannot currently build, and a flat point field reads exactly the same with a trivial static fallback. That is a deliberate swap, flagged for a later call if the WebGL version is wanted.

The discipline through all of it was that motion has to be optional. I could not open a browser on this machine for the whole stretch, so I built every animation to degrade to a working static page rather than assume it would. Numbers render their final value before any script runs, so no-JS and reduced-motion both show the real figure. Reveals are visible by default and only hide once the code has confirmed it can bring them back. The cross-highlight is a shared hover with a no-op default, so the chart and the table still render alone. The view transition falls back to a normal navigation when the API is missing. Reduced motion turns the whole lot off, count-ups, reveals, the drifting field, and the page transitions.

Then the hardening. The favicon is the oxblood Q, drawn as a ring and a tail so it needs no font. There is an Open Graph card and the social and search metadata behind it, a robots file that points at a sitemap of the stable routes, a skip link to a focusable main landmark, and a reduced-motion pass over the new work. I had the polish reviewed the way I review everything, aimed at the failures you cannot see by clicking: does it degrade, does it leak, does reduced motion reach all of it. It earned its place again. It found a count-up whose animation frame kept firing after the element unmounted, a reveal that could strand content hidden if the observer ever failed to construct, a cross-highlight that missed the top bucket on an inclusive bound, and a deploy footgun where a missing site-url would have shipped localhost links to every scraper. All fixed. One honest leftover: the stack lists a motion library I ended up not needing, since canvas, a scroll observer, and GSAP covered the work, so it sits unused rather than pulled, because I would not churn the lockfile on a machine that cannot rebuild it.

## The grail turn, and filing footwear as footwear

Narrowed the whole effort to a target set of about eighteen grails, the stable-silhouette models that carry the resale volume and are the pieces people search by name: the Rick Owens Ramones and Geobasket, the Balenciaga Triple S and Track and Speed, the Margiela Tabi and GAT and Future, the Saint Laurent Wyatt and Teddy and L01, the Dior Luster denim and Navigate boot, the Vetements DHL tee. Encoded them into a small grail_targets table with an alt-spelling dictionary and a replica-hazard flag, and pointed the resolver, the corpus pull, and the reference set for later image work all at the same list.

The reason to go deep rather than wide was a bug the public site was already showing. A GAT and a Tabi were filed under denim and a Ramones under a top, three of the most recognizable shoes in menswear sorted as if they were trousers and shirts. The cause was mine and it was subtle. To de-bias the corpus off footwear I had seeded some broadening searches like "maison margiela denim", and the resolver was reading a row's archetype from the search keyword as well as the title. So a real GAT that came back under that denim search inherited denim, and because a footwear model's own archetype only filled in when nothing else had, the wrong keyword archetype won. The fix is small and it is the right shape: a resolved model's own garment is authoritative, GAT and Tabi and Ramones are footwear no matter what search dragged them in, and the keyword archetype is kept only for rows that never resolve to a model, which is where that de-bias was actually meant to help. I was careful to leave the genuinely ambiguous names out of it. Margiela Replica is a whole capsule, shoes and jackets both, so a Replica jacket still reads its garment from the title rather than being forced to footwear.

While I was in the resolver I fixed a second thing the marts were hiding: the same physical listing could reach a piece's comps twice, once under each search keyword that returned it, and double-count. So the cleaning step now keeps one row per marketplace, piece, size, whole-dollar price and normalized title, a fingerprint tight enough that two genuinely different sales almost never collide, and it stays within a marketplace so a piece that truly sold on both sides keeps both for the cross-marketplace comparison. It dropped twenty seven re-surfaced duplicates, including the doubled comps I had seen on the Future page.

Then the pull, one targeted search per grail on both marketplaces, and for the first time I kept the listing images, which the adapters had been throwing away at the boundary. The payoff is the number the whole project turns on. Model-level resolution across the corpus went from about seven percent to about a quarter, on the grail brands specifically from about a fifth to about half, and the cross-marketplace spread mart, the same-piece-in-two-places table that is the point of all this, went from five pieces to fifteen, and those fifteen are the actual grails: GAT, Future, Tabi, Wyatt, Triple S, Speed, Ramones, Geobasket, L01, Luster, Teddy, Track. The pieces carry deep grade-A comps now, a hundred and twenty two GAT sales, ninety Futures, eighty six Tabis, rather than the handful they had before.

The frontend got the corrections that cost credibility with this audience. The read layer had been title-casing the normalized brand token, which mangles exactly the names these buyers know, so "number n ine" and "comme des garcons" and "enfants riches deprimes" were showing up as nonsense; there is a real display map now, with the cedilla and the accents and the stylized ALYX and the parenthetical in Number (N)ine. Piece identity reads as three tiers, the archetype as a kicker over the brand over the model, and the model line simply vanishes when there is no model rather than printing "Unspecified model" over a piece the archetype already names. Pulled the styleguide route out of the public site and trimmed the colophon.

What I will not dress up. Three of the grail brands are season-driven, not model-driven, and the reference report said so up front: Number (N)ine, Undercover, and Dior Homme attach their value to specific collections and motifs, not repeated models. Their model-level resolution is low, a few percent for Number (N)ine and Undercover, and that is honest rather than broken. I kept them in the set, indexed by their recurring motifs where I could, and graded their confidence low rather than inventing a model to hit a number.

## Arbitrage, and only comparing one object

The mispricing detector had been written since the price-model phase and sitting inert, because it reads a table of active asks that stays empty until a pull fills it, and that pull spends. Wired the ingestion path it needed. A live search on Grailed with the sold filter off returns listings still for sale, and the current ask is the listing price rather than a realized sale, so it gets its own canonical shape and its own small adapter that skips the sold rows and reads the ask. The resolver stamps those active rows with a piece the same way it does sold rows, and the whole thing surfaces through a mart rather than the frontend, because the site is a thin read layer and the logic belongs in the pipeline. The mart is the honest version of the idea: an active ask below a piece's tenth-percentile sold price, ranked by how far under the sold median it sits, carrying the comp count and the confidence grade the floor rests on.

One approved pull lit it up, about seven hundred active asks, and the detector found eight. Then the first pass taught me something I should have seen coming. The strongest signal was a Saint Laurent piece ninety two percent under, an ask of seventy two dollars against a five-hundred-dollar floor, and it was garbage. The piece had no model, it was a coarse brand-and-archetype bucket pooling every kind of Saint Laurent item of that garment, so a cheap one looked wildly underpriced against a blended median of expensive ones. It was the exact mistake the spread mart had already taught me once: a brand-and-archetype bucket is not one object. So arbitrage compares an ask only to model-level pieces now, the same guard, and the false signal fell away, leaving eight real ones, a Dior Navigate seventy one percent under, a Triple S at sixty, a Wyatt, an L01, a Ramones, each a genuine live ask sitting under a floor built from deep comps of the same shoe.

The framing is deliberate and it rides in the data rather than the copy. An ask under the sold floor is not a promised profit; it can be a fake, the wrong size, or a stale listing, and on the most-faked classes, the Triple S and the GAT and the DHL tee, that caveat is not hypothetical. The per-piece flag and the underpriced view both say so plainly and show the comp count and grade so the reader can judge how firm the floor is.

Two honest leftovers. It is Grailed only, because the eBay actor I have pinned returns sold listings by design, so eBay active asks would need their own actor and their own pull, and I would rather ship one honest side than fake a second. And active listings go stale the moment they sell, so this is a snapshot, not a feed; keeping it live would be a small recurring pull on a schedule, which is a spend decision rather than something to switch on quietly.

## Seeing the shoe, and refusing to name what it cannot

Added a way to identify a grail from a photo, and drew a hard line around it first. Resolution stays text-first; the images never touch the canonical key or how a piece is identified from a listing. Visual search is a separate thing bolted on the side: image-to-image retrieval over the set I have already resolved by text, so a photo finds the nearest reference pieces and hands back their real comps. It is zero-shot, an off-the-shelf fashion CLIP model with no fine-tuning, because the point of v1 was to measure what off-the-shelf actually buys before spending anything on training.

The shape holds the same discipline as the rest of the machine. The model runs in a stateless worker on a GPU that scales to zero, so the meter only moves while it is embedding, and the weights are baked into the image so a cold start skips the download rather than paying it every time. The worker takes image URLs and returns vectors, nothing else; it owns no data. The pipeline owns the database write, and the reference vectors live in Postgres with pgvector, stored as half-width floats under a cosine index, which is the right trade for a set this small. A one-time backfill embedded all nine hundred forty three reference images, every one, no failures. The query path resizes the photo in the browser and posts the bytes to the server, which hands them straight to the embedding worker and never stores them anywhere. I went the long way to get here. First a direct browser-to-storage upload, which leans on a completion callback that is sensitive to deployment config and failed opaquely in production. Then a server-side upload, which ran into the blob store being configured for private access while the code asked for public. Both were me treating a throwaway query image as something that needs storage, and it does not. So it does not touch storage at all now: the resized bytes are embedded in flight and the vector is the only thing that outlives the request. Resizing in the browser keeps a raw phone photo from ever traveling whole and quietly turns a HEIC into something the model can read, and dropping the blob dependency took a store, a token, a cleanup cron, and a whole class of failure out of the picture. The one honest gap is that the identify endpoint is public and not rate limited, so it can be made to burn GPU time; that is the nature of an open upload-and-identify, and the scale-to-zero worker and small caps bound the cost rather than remove it.

Then the part that decides whether any of it ships, which is measuring it honestly. There is no clean fresh-photo holdout, because the active-listing pull kept no images, so I measured leave-one-out on the reference set: every image queried against all the others, asking whether the nearest matches share its brand, its model, and its exact piece. The nearest match lands the right brand about ninety five percent of the time, the right model about ninety three, and the exact piece about eighty six; widen to the top ten and brand reaches ninety nine. That ordering is the whole story and it is the honest one, so the interface asserts brand and type, offers the model only as a hedge, and never claims the season. I calibrated the confidence bands off the actual distance distribution rather than picking numbers that looked good, and a photo that is not one of the eighteen comes back as no strong match instead of a forced guess. The number is an upper bound and I say so on the page: query and reference are both resale listing photos, so a real photo in bad light does worse, worst on the soft items the grail report already flagged, Dior denim and Undercover graphics, where the model rank drops into the eighties and seventies. And it cannot tell a real from a good replica, which on the Triple S and the GAT and the DHL tee is the thing a buyer most wants to know, so those carry the caveat in plain sight.
